1early 10,000 years after three tribal fishermen were buried in what is now 
the ritzy enclave of La Jolla Farms ‚Äî just west of the Muir College campus ‚Äî their bones have yet to find a final resting place.
Ever since the remains were uncovered by archeology students from 
Cal State Northridge in 1976, the UCSD administration and the Kumeyaay 
nation ‚Äî an American-Indian tribe that hails from Southern California and Northern Mexico ‚Äî have been fighting over the rights to the bones. Though the two parties reached a stalemate last year after the university refused to deem the remains ‚Äúculturally affiliated‚Äù with the Kumeyaay nation, recent unrest on campus has rekindled the dispute. 
The Black Student Union‚Äôs list of demands, released three weeks ago to 
address racial insensitivity at UCSD, included a request that the university cooperate with the Kumeyaay nation. Now, in a joint statement with the BSU on Mar. 4, Chancellor Marye Anne Fox has pledged a commitment to continue working toward a resolution with the Kumeyaay nation.
The bones are some of the oldest ever found in the Western Hemisphere. 
They were unearthed from the land beneath Chancellor Fox‚Äôs University House ‚Äî purchased by UCSD in 1967 as an official residence for chancel-lors, and to host events and fundraisers. Because the Northridge archeology students discovered the remains on university

Since their discovery, the artifacts have been shipped to various locations across the country, displayed at prestigious museums like the Smithsonian and studied with particular scrutiny for their age ‚Äî all amid pleas by local American-Indians to have the ancestral remains returned and reburied. 
According to the Native-American Graves Protection and Repatriation Act of 
1990, all institutions that receive federal funding must return American-Indian cul-tural artifacts and human remains when a tribe can prove ‚Äúcultural affiliation. ‚Äù
The law stipulates that an artifact is culturally affiliated with a tribe‚Äôs heritage if 
there is evidence of a connection ‚Äúbased on geographical, kinship, biological, archeo-logical, linguistic, folklore, oral tradition, historical evidence or other information or expert opinion. ‚Äù
The Kumeyaay Cultural Repatriations Committee ‚Äî a group formed by the San

According to members of the committee, though 29 sets of bones were dug up in 1976, only the three in question are accounted for.
